## LinkedIn

A founder I'd been talking to sent me a LinkedIn post he was about to publish. He asked if it was good.

I read the first three sentences. Then I read the next three. Then I told him ChatGPT had written it.

He paused and said, well, I told it what I wanted to say, and it wrote it for me. Same thing, right?

It wasn't the same thing.

The question wasn't "is this post good." The post was technically fine. The question that was actually being asked was: should I use AI to write things that have my name on them?

That question doesn't have one answer. It has different answers for different pieces of writing.

Most writing a founder does falls into one of three jobs.

Operational: internal docs, FAQ pages, instructions for a contractor. The reader doesn't care who wrote it. They care it's accurate. AI is good at this. Use it.

Informational: blog posts that explain a concept, how-to guides, summaries of research. The reader cares the information is right. AI can do most of the work, but rewrite the voice-heavy parts in your own words.

Relational: founder posts, sales emails to a relationship-stage prospect, the newsletter that goes to your community. The reader is paying attention to who you sound like. AI can't do this. Not because the model isn't smart enough. Because the reader can tell, and the second they can tell, the trust is gone.

The founder I was talking to had used AI for a post that was doing a relational job. The post was about a hard decision he'd made in his business. The reason readers would have engaged was to feel like they were inside his head. AI gave them a generic founder's head.

The work was wrong for the job.

Two years ago AI-written content was a novelty. Most readers couldn't tell. That window has closed. Readers in 2026 are calibrated. They're tired. They're starving for writing that sounds like a person made it. If you're a founder, that's an opportunity, not a problem.

Use AI where AI is the right tool. Write yourself where your voice is the point.

Most of the time you'll know which is which by the third sentence, if you're willing to ask.

## Newsletter

Hi friends,

A founder I'd been talking to sent me a LinkedIn post he was about to publish. He asked if it was good.

I read the first three sentences. Then the next three. Then I told him ChatGPT had written it.

He paused and said, well, I told it what I wanted to say, and it wrote it for me. Same thing, right?

It wasn't the same thing. The conversation we had after that has shaped almost every conversation I've had with founders about AI since.

The question wasn't "is this post good." The post was fine. The question being asked was: should I use AI to write things that have my name on them?

That question doesn't have one answer. It has different answers for different pieces of writing. And the founders who get this right are the ones who've stopped asking *should I use AI* and started asking *what is this piece of writing actually doing.*

Most writing a founder does falls into three jobs.

The first is operational. Internal docs, FAQ pages, instructions for a contractor. The reader doesn't care who wrote it. They care it's accurate and clear. AI is good at this. Use it.

The second is informational. Blog posts that explain a concept, how-to guides, summaries of research. The reader cares that the information is right. AI can do most of the work, but rewrite the voice-heavy parts in your own words.

The third is relational. Founder posts. Sales emails to a relationship-stage prospect. The newsletter going to your community. The story you're telling about why your business exists. The reader is paying attention to *who you sound like*. AI can't do this. Not because the model isn't smart enough. Because the reader can tell, and the second they can tell, the trust is gone.

The founder I was talking to had used AI for a post doing a relational job. The reason readers would have engaged was to feel like they were inside his head. AI gave them a generic founder's head. The work was wrong for the job.

Here's the simple test I use: read the opening paragraph aloud. Then read a paragraph from a text you sent a friend yesterday. If the rhythm and word choices are noticeably different, you wrote two different people. If the relational version sounds like the AI-flavored one, the post is going to land flat.

Two years ago AI content was a novelty. That window has closed. Readers in 2026 are calibrated. They're starving for writing that sounds like a person made it. If you're a founder, that's an opportunity.

Use AI where AI is the right tool. Write yourself where your voice is the point.
