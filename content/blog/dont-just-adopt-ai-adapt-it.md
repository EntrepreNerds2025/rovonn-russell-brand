Most founders I talk to right now have at least four AI subscriptions sitting in browser tabs they haven't opened in three weeks. ChatGPT. Claude. Maybe Jasper. Maybe a niche tool they bought because someone on LinkedIn swore it would change everything. None of them are doing the thing the founder thought they'd do.

The founders aren't lazy. They're not behind. They're not slow.

They just adopted AI when they should have ADAPTed it. And those two words are doing very different jobs.

## The squeeze nobody's naming

Here's what's actually happening in May 2026. Big companies are using AI to cut roles. The five largest professional services firms have collectively reduced their headcount by 11% in the last 18 months. Software costs are up 30% across the SaaS stack most small businesses depend on. Contractor rates have climbed faster than client budgets. Tariffs and input costs have eaten the cushion most owners didn't know they had.

Meanwhile, the small businesses and founders who haven't figured out how to use AI for their specific work are absorbing all of it. Costs up. Revenue flat or down. Margin compressed. And the founder is still the one answering emails on Saturday morning.

This is the squeeze. And it doesn't get better by hoping AI is a fad. It gets worse, fast, while you wait.

## Three responses to the squeeze (only one wins)

Most founders I see take one of three paths from here.

The first is defense. They reject AI. They complain about it on LinkedIn. They say it's not for their industry. They wait for the bubble to pop. And while they're waiting, the founder down the street who didn't pretend is undercutting their pricing, delivering faster, and taking customers they used to win. Defense looks like protecting your business. It's actually losing customers, gone.

The second is watching. This is the most common path because it looks safe. You don't have an opinion yet. You'll look into AI next quarter. The problem is by next quarter, the founder who started six months ago has installed three workflows you haven't. You're now competing on a slope you can't climb back up. Watching looks like neutrality. It's the napkin, still in the drawer.

The third is offense. This is the only path that wins. But here's the trap: offense isn't "adopt every AI tool you see on Twitter." If you do that, you end up with four subscriptions sitting in browser tabs and zero hours back in your week. Offense is something more specific. Offense is ADAPT.

## The difference between adopt and ADAPT

Adopting AI is easy. Anyone can buy a subscription.

ADAPTing AI is the work most founders skip. Which is why most founders are still working Saturdays, eating the rising costs of every tool and every contractor, and watching the version of the business they actually wanted stay stuck on a napkin in a drawer.

ADAPT is a five-step diagnostic. Each letter does a specific job. Most founders skip three of the five steps. That's why nothing sticks.

**A is Assess.** What does your business actually need? Where are you bleeding time? What can only YOU currently do? Where would AI be a fit, and where will it backfire? The Assess step is the one most founders skip first, because it feels slow. You can't install anything in the Assess phase. You're just looking at your own business with honest eyes. But everything that comes after depends on this step being done well.

**D is Discover.** What tools, agents, or systems actually match the gaps the assessment found? This is the step where most founders START. They open Product Hunt, see five AI tools, install three of them, and try to figure out how they fit. The order is backwards. You don't discover tools and then look for problems. You assess the problem and then look for tools that fit it.

**A is Apply.** Pick what fits THIS specific work. Tailor, don't template. The wrong AI is worse than no AI. A generic content tool installed without a voice training step will produce generic content that doesn't sound like you, which damages your brand. A scheduling tool installed without a routing logic will book the wrong meetings, which damages your week. Apply is about narrowing, not expanding.

**P is Produce.** Ship the system. Run it. See what actually delivers versus what looked good on paper. Most founders also skip this step, because they keep adjusting the system before they've actually used it. Pick one workflow. Run it for two weeks. Measure. THEN refine. Don't pre-optimize.

**T is Transform.** What does the business look like when the system runs without you? That's the test of whether you ADAPTed AI or just added it. If you still have to babysit the workflow every day, you didn't ADAPT it. You bought another full-time job for yourself.

## What ADAPT looks like in real numbers

Here's an example. A founder running a 4-person interior design studio in Vancouver fills out The Edge form on my site. Her time eater is client proposals. Each proposal takes her 6 hours. Her stalled project is an online course teaching homeowners how to plan renovations themselves. She's been meaning to record it for two years.

Most "AI advisors" would tell her to install a generic proposal generator. That's the Discover step done first. It would produce proposals that sound like a template, lose her clients, and add another monthly bill to her stack.

The ADAPT version looks different. We Assess: the bottleneck isn't proposals generally. It's the customization of proposals to each specific client. That customization is what wins her business. So a generic tool would actually kill the thing that's working.

We Discover: we look for tools that can be voice-trained on her writing and her past proposals.

We Apply: with my team at EntrepreNerds, we install what amounts to a Virtual Proposal Writer for her business. Claude trained on her past 30 proposals as voice samples, paired with a template that handles the customization layer. Time-to-install: about 3 hours.

We Produce: she runs the workflow for two weeks. Proposal time drops from 6 hours to 90 minutes. That's 18 hours back per month at her current proposal volume.

We Transform: with 18 hours back per month, she records the online course she'd been parking for two years. That course launches and starts generating its own revenue. The business isn't just running better. It's running a fundamentally different shape. She effectively just hired a Virtual Proposal Writer for the cost of a Claude subscription.

That's ADAPT. None of it would have happened if she'd just adopted a proposal generator.

## The framework as the wedge

ADAPT isn't a content recipe. It's not a storytelling methodology. It's not a list of AI tools. It's a diagnostic that figures out which AI fits which business, in which order, with what tradeoffs. The output varies. Sometimes it's a content engine. Sometimes it's an AI agent. Sometimes it's a delegation layer. Sometimes it's brand and messaging work. The framework is the spine. The deliverable depends on what the Assess step surfaced.

What stays constant is the order. Assess before Discover. Apply before Produce. Produce before Transform. Most founders run this in the wrong order, which is why most AI investments don't stick.

## Three paths, one question

Defense costs you customers you used to win. Watching costs you the napkin, still in the drawer. Offense is the only path that wins, and ADAPT is how you do offense without wasting another year and another four subscriptions.

So the question isn't whether AI is going to affect your business. It already is.

The question is which one you're on.
