A few weeks ago I typed six bullet points about a community night into an AI tool and asked it to write the recap. Fifteen seconds. It came back clean, grammatical, organized into a tidy little arc.

And completely dead.

What was missing wasn't words. The grammar was better than mine. The structure was fine. What was missing was the kid who stayed after everyone left to stack the chairs without being asked. The way the room went quiet when the quietest person in it finally spoke. The thing that actually happened.

The AI couldn't know any of that. It wasn't in the room. It can only work with what I noticed and chose to hand it. And in my bullet points, I hadn't handed it the moment. I'd handed it a summary.

## Writing was never the scarce part

For a long time the bottleneck on telling a story was the writing. You had the moment in your head, and the work was getting it onto the page in a way that did it justice. That's the part most people found hard. That's the part they hired out.

That part is now close to free.

Anyone can generate competent words about anything in fifteen seconds. Which means competent words are worth roughly what they cost, which is nothing. The thing that's suddenly scarce is the thing that was never the bottleneck, because everyone assumed it was easy: noticing what actually mattered.

It is not easy. Noticing is a skill. It's the skill of being present enough in a room to catch the one detail that carries the whole meaning, and disciplined enough to write it down before it evaporates. No model can do it for you, because the model wasn't there.

This is the shift, and most people have it backwards. They think AI makes the storyteller obsolete. It does the opposite. It commoditizes everything around the storyteller and leaves the one human part standing in the open, more valuable than it has ever been.

## Story is the moat. AI is the engine.

I build AI systems for a living. Agents that triage inboxes, research pipelines, content engines that ship without me touching them. I'm not romantic about this. The tools are real and they're staying.

But here's what a year of building them taught me. The leverage isn't in the model. It's in what you feed it. A content engine is only as good as the moments going into it, and the moments come from a human who was paying attention. Make the inputs lifeless and the most sophisticated system on earth gives you lifeless output, faster.

So the work splits cleanly. AI is the engine: it drafts, it shapes, it scales, it does the part that used to take all the time. Story is the moat: it's the noticing, the judgment about what's worth telling, the voice that makes a stranger care. The engine is for rent to anyone. The moat is yours.

It's not that AI made storytelling matter less. It's that AI made it the only part that's hard to copy.

## What this means if your work matters

If you're a founder, this is the argument for keeping your hand in the part of the story only you can tell. Automate the production. Do not automate the noticing. The reason people buy from you and not the identical competitor is the thing you saw that they didn't bother to.

If you run a mission-driven organization, it's the same truth wearing different clothes. Funders have sat through a thousand decks of outputs. Sessions delivered, people served, dollars spent. What they remember is one young person, one specific moment, told honestly enough that they feel it. The numbers prove the work. The moment is what makes anyone care that it got proven. AI can format the report in seconds. It cannot be in the room when the change happens. That's still your job, and it's the one that earns the renewal.

The tool got faster. The bar didn't move. It just got clearer where the bar actually is.

## The ten-minute habit

Here's the only practice that matters, and it costs nothing. Once a week, before you write anything, spend ten minutes writing down what you noticed. Not the wins. The moments. The person who stayed late. The sentence that changed the room. The detail you almost walked past.

That list is the one thing AI cannot generate for you. Everything else, the drafting, the formatting, the scaling, hand it to the engine. The noticing stays with you. It always did. The machines just made it obvious.

Big respect to everyone doing work that's worth noticing, and writing it down before it disappears. That's the whole craft now. Looking forward to sharing more of how I'm building the engine around it.
