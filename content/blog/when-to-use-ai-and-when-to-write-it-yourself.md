A founder I'd been talking to sent me a LinkedIn post he was about to publish. He asked if it was good.

I read the first three sentences. Then I read the next three. Then I told him ChatGPT had written it.

He paused and said, well, I told it what I wanted to say, and it wrote it for me. Same thing, right?

It wasn't the same thing. I told him why, and that conversation has shaped almost every conversation I've had with founders about AI since.

## The question that was actually being asked

The question wasn't "is this post good." The post was technically fine. It had a hook, a structure, a useful idea. If you didn't know him, you'd read it and think okay, makes sense.

The question that was actually being asked was: should I use AI to write things that have my name on them?

That question doesn't have one answer. It has different answers for different pieces of writing. And the founders who get this right are the ones who've stopped asking *should I use AI* and started asking *what is this piece of writing actually doing.*

## What writing is doing

Most writing a founder does falls into one of three jobs.

The first job is *operational*. Internal docs, FAQ pages, meeting notes, instructions for a contractor, a description of how a process works. The reader doesn't care who wrote it. They care that it's accurate and clear. AI is good at this. Sometimes it's better than you, because you'd rush it. The work needs structure, not voice. Use AI.

The second job is *informational*. Blog posts that explain a concept, how-to guides, technical breakdowns, summaries of research. The reader cares that the information is right and the explanation is clear. They mostly don't care whether you specifically wrote it. AI can do most of the work here, but you should still rewrite the voice-heavy parts in your own words. Use AI as a co-writer, not as the writer.

The third job is *relational*. The reader is deciding whether to trust you. Founder posts. Sales emails to a relationship-stage prospect. The newsletter that goes to your community. The story you're telling about why your business exists. The part of your website that explains who you are. Anything where the reader is paying attention to *who you sound like*. AI can't do this. Not because the model isn't smart enough. Because the reader can tell, and the second they can tell, the trust is gone.

The founder I was talking to had used AI for a post that was doing a relational job. The post was about a hard decision he'd made in his business. The reason readers would have engaged with it was to feel like they were inside his head. AI gave them something that sounded like *a generic founder's head*. The work was wrong for the job.

## When to use AI: the Assess and Discover question, applied to one piece

This is what [the ADAPT framework](/frameworks/adapt) looks like applied to a single sentence-level decision. Assess: what is this piece of writing doing? Discover: what's the right tool for that specific job?

Most founders skip both phases. They open ChatGPT, paste a topic, take what comes out, run it through a polish prompt, and ship it. The tool got the answer fast. The job got done. The reader walks away with a slightly diluted version of who you actually are.

The fix isn't to never use AI for writing. The fix is to ask the assessment question every time you sit down: *is this operational, informational, or relational?* If it's operational, lean on AI. If it's informational, draft with AI and rewrite the voice. If it's relational, write it yourself. The ten extra minutes pay back ten times over in trust.

## A simple test

When you've drafted something, with or without AI, read the opening paragraph aloud. Then read a paragraph from a text message you sent a friend yesterday. If the rhythm and word choices are noticeably different, you wrote two different people. If the relational version sounds like the AI-flavored one, the post is going to land flat. The reader's gut catches the mismatch even if their eyes don't.

The founders I work with who write the strongest content all do some version of this check. Most of them don't even realize they're doing it. The ones whose content lands flat are usually skipping it. ## Why this matters more in 2026 than it did in 2024

Two years ago, AI-written content was a novelty. Most readers couldn't reliably tell. The market hadn't built the immune response yet.

That window has closed. Readers in 2026 (especially the ones who'd hire you, buy from you, refer you) have read a lot of AI content. They're tired. They're calibrated. The signal-to-noise advantage of writing in your actual voice has gone up, not down. People are starving for writing that sounds like a person made it. If you're a founder, that's an opportunity, not a problem.

Use AI where AI is the right tool. Write yourself where your voice is the point. Most of the time you'll know which is which by the third sentence, if you're willing to ask.
